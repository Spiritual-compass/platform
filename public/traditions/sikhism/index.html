🪯 Sikhism — The Path of the Guru

Born in the Punjab five centuries ago, Sikhism teaches that there is one God, all humans are equal, and the highest service is service to others. The Guru Granth Sahib is its eternal living guide.

The Sikh Vision

Sikhism emerged in 15th-century Punjab when Guru Nanak received a divine revelation and declared "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim." His vision: a single, formless God accessible to all people without priestly mediation — and a life of prayer, honest work, and service as the highest spiritual practice.

☝️ Ik Onkar — One God

The Mool Mantar, the opening verse of the Guru Granth Sahib, begins: "Ik Onkar" — One God. The divine is formless, beyond gender and image, permeating all creation. Sikhs do not worship idols; they direct devotion toward the formless Waheguru (Wonderful Lord), encountered through scripture, meditation on the Name, and loving service.

🤝 Equality of All People

Sikhism was revolutionary in rejecting the caste system at a time when it structured every aspect of South Asian life. Guru Nanak taught that all human beings carry the divine light equally — there is no spiritual superiority by birth, gender, or class. The Langar (free community kitchen) embodies this: everyone sits together and eats together, regardless of who they are.

📖 The Living Guru

In 1708, the tenth Guru, Gobind Singh, declared that after him the succession of human Gurus would end. The Guru Granth Sahib — the sacred scripture — would be the eternal, living Guru. The scripture is treated with the reverence due a living teacher: it is carried in procession, installed on a throne (takht), and read continuously in Gurdwaras around the world.

🛡️ Courage and Justice

Sikhism does not counsel passive withdrawal from the world. The Sikh ideal is the Sant-Sipahi — the saint-soldier — one who is devoted in prayer and courageous in the face of injustice. Guru Gobind Singh institutionalised this through the Khalsa brotherhood, whose members are committed to defending the weak and upholding righteousness (Dharam) regardless of personal cost.

The Ten Gurus

Sikhism was shaped by ten human Gurus over 239 years. Sikhs believe the divine light of Guru Nanak passed uninterrupted through each successor — ten bodies, one Light.

1st Guru · 1469–1539

Guru Nanak Dev Ji

The founder. After a mystical experience in which he was taken into the divine presence, he emerged saying "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim." He travelled to Mecca, Baghdad, Tibet, and across India, teaching the oneness of God and the equality of all people through poetry and song.

2nd Guru · 1504–1552

Guru Angad Dev Ji

Standardised the Gurmukhi script, the alphabet in which the Guru Granth Sahib is written, making scripture accessible to ordinary people regardless of their knowledge of Sanskrit or Arabic. He also collected Guru Nanak's hymns and institutionalised the Langar.

3rd Guru · 1479–1574

Guru Amar Das Ji

Abolished purdah (the veiling of women) and sati (widow immolation), emphasising the full spiritual equality of women. Organised the Sikh community into 22 districts (manjis) to spread the teaching and instituted Anand Karaj, the Sikh marriage ceremony.

4th Guru · 1534–1581

Guru Ram Das Ji

Founded the city of Amritsar and began excavation of the sacred pool (Amrit Sarovar) around which the Golden Temple would later be built. Composed the Lavan — the four verses recited during Sikh wedding ceremonies, circling the Guru Granth Sahib.

5th Guru · 1563–1606

Guru Arjan Dev Ji

Compiled the first edition of the Sikh scripture, the Adi Granth, including the hymns of the first four Gurus, his own compositions, and verses from Hindu and Muslim saints (including Kabir and Sheikh Farid). He completed the Golden Temple. The first Sikh martyr, executed by the Mughal Emperor Jahangir.

6th Guru · 1595–1644

Guru Hargobind Ji

Introduced the concept of Miri-Piri — two swords representing temporal and spiritual authority. He built the Akal Takht (Throne of the Timeless) opposite the Golden Temple as the seat of Sikh political authority. Established that Sikhs must be capable of defending themselves and the innocent.

7th Guru · 1630–1664

Guru Har Rai Ji

Known for his gentle, compassionate nature and deep love of nature. He maintained a large army but avoided direct conflict with the Mughals while continuing to expand the Sikh community. Established a hospital and herb garden, using healing as a form of seva (service).

8th Guru · 1656–1664

Guru Har Krishan Ji

Became Guru at the age of five — the youngest. He died at eight of smallpox contracted while serving the sick during an epidemic in Delhi. His last words pointed toward the next Guru: "Baba Bakale." He is remembered as a symbol of selfless service and innocence.

9th Guru · 1621–1675

Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji

The second Sikh martyr. When Kashmiri Pandits (Hindus) facing forced conversion appealed to him for help, he offered himself in their place — protecting the right of Hindus to practice their own faith, even at the cost of his life. Executed in Delhi by order of the Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb. He is called "Hind di Chadar" — the Shield of India.

10th Guru · 1666–1708

Guru Gobind Singh Ji

The last human Guru. In 1699 he founded the Khalsa — the community of initiated Sikhs — and gave them the five Ks and the shared surname Singh (lion) for men and Kaur (princess) for women. A warrior, poet, and scholar, he declared that after him, the Guru Granth Sahib would be the eternal Guru of the Sikhs.

The Guru Granth Sahib

The Guru Granth Sahib is the eternal, living Guru of the Sikhs — treated not as a historical document but as a living teacher present in every Gurdwara (Sikh place of worship) in the world.

📜

Composition and Structure

1,430 pages of devotional poetry, composed by six of the ten Sikh Gurus and 36 other saints — including the Muslim Sufi poet Baba Farid and the Hindu bhakti poet Kabir. Written in Gurmukhi script in various languages, the scripture is organised by raga (musical mode) and covers themes of devotion, ethics, and the nature of God.

🙏

Reverence in Practice

In Gurdwaras, the Guru Granth Sahib is installed on a throne (takht) under a canopy (chandoa). A Granthi (reader) fans it with a chauri (whisk) as a sign of royal honour. When transported, it is carried on the head. It is put to rest each evening and "woken" each morning with ceremony. The akhand path — a continuous reading of the entire scripture — takes 48 hours.

🎵

Kirtan — Sacred Music

The verses of the Guru Granth Sahib are meant to be sung. Kirtan — devotional singing of scriptural hymns, often accompanied by harmonium and tabla — is the primary form of Sikh worship. Guru Nanak himself sang his revelations with his Muslim companion Mardana on the rabab. Kirtan is considered a form of meditation that opens the heart to divine grace.

"There is but One God. His name is Truth. He is the Creator, fearless, without hatred, timeless, self-existent, the Great Enlightener."
— Guru Nanak, Mool Mantar (opening of the Guru Granth Sahib)

The Khalsa and the Five Ks

On Vaisakhi day 1699, Guru Gobind Singh called the Sikhs together and created the Khalsa — the community of the pure. Initiated Khalsa Sikhs bear five articles of faith, each beginning with the Punjabi letter "K" (Kakkar).

1st Kakkar

Kesh — Uncut Hair

Hair left in its natural state, covered by a turban (dastar) for men and typically a chunni (scarf) for women. Kesh represents acceptance of God's will and the natural form given at birth. The turban is not merely a covering but a crown of courage and dignity.

2nd Kakkar

Kangha — Wooden Comb

A small wooden comb worn in the hair. It represents cleanliness and the importance of keeping one's spiritual life orderly and maintained. The comb is used twice daily when hair is tied — a physical reminder of disciplined practice.

3rd Kakkar

Kara — Steel Bracelet

A circular steel bracelet worn on the dominant wrist. The circle represents the infinite, formless God. Steel represents strength and permanence. It serves as a constant physical reminder of one's commitment to Sikh values and the bond with the Guru.

4th Kakkar

Kachera — Cotton Undergarment

A specific style of cotton undergarment symbolising self-restraint, modesty, and readiness for action. Historically practical for a warrior community, it represents moral discipline and the importance of maintaining dignity in all circumstances.

5th Kakkar

Kirpan — Steel Sword

A steel sword or knife — the symbol of justice, courage, and the commitment to defend the vulnerable. The kirpan is not a weapon of aggression but of moral authority. It is worn as a reminder that Sikhs are called to oppose tyranny and protect all people, regardless of their faith.

Core Practices

Sikh life is structured around three core commitments, summed up in the phrase Naam Japo, Kirat Karo, Vand Chhako — Meditate on God's Name, Earn an Honest Living, Share with Others.

🙏

Naam Simran — Remembrance of God

Meditation on the Name of God — most often the word Waheguru ("Wonderful Lord"). Practised in the Amrit Vela (the hours before dawn), through repetition (jaap), through kirtan, and through continuous awareness throughout the day. The goal is to dissolve the ego (haumai) and let divine love flow through the self.

🍽️

Langar — The Free Kitchen

Every Gurdwara in the world operates a free kitchen (Langar) where everyone — of any faith, caste, gender, or background — is welcome to eat together as equals. The Golden Temple in Amritsar feeds 100,000 people daily, entirely through voluntary service (seva) and donations. Langar is the most visible embodiment of Sikh teaching on equality and generosity.

🤝

Seva — Selfless Service

Service to others, offered without expectation of reward, as a form of divine worship. Seva in the Gurdwara — cooking, cleaning, serving in the Langar — is considered as spiritually valuable as meditation. In times of disaster, Sikh relief organisations (like the Khalsa Aid) are often among the first responders, offering food and aid to anyone in need regardless of religion.

"Sach kaho, sach suno, sach ki karni karni — Speak truth, hear truth, live in truth."
— Guru Granth Sahib

Sikhism Today

With approximately 30 million adherents worldwide, Sikhism is the world's fifth-largest religion. The Punjabi diaspora has carried the tradition to every continent. The Golden Temple in Amritsar remains one of the world's great pilgrimage sites — open to visitors of all faiths, welcoming and feeding all who come. Sikhs are recognisable by the turban and the five Ks, and known for their commitment to service, justice, and hospitality.
